Quartz vs. Granite: Which Countertop Fits Your Home?

Homeowner ResourceClear, practical guidance

Quartz and granite can both produce durable, attractive countertops. The better choice depends on how you cook, the appearance you prefer and how much maintenance you are willing to perform.

How they are made

Granite is natural stone cut into slabs, so each piece has unique mineral patterns and variation. Quartz surfaces are engineered from mineral aggregate, resin and pigments, which allows more controlled color and pattern consistency.

Appearance

Choose granite when natural variation and one-of-a-kind movement are priorities. Choose quartz when you want predictable samples, consistent background color or patterns designed to coordinate with specific cabinets and tile.

Maintenance

Many granite products benefit from periodic sealing, depending on the stone and sealer. Quartz is generally nonporous, but it still requires appropriate cleaning and protection from harsh chemicals.

Heat and impact

Natural stone generally handles heat well, but thermal shock and concentrated heat can still cause damage. Quartz contains resin and should be protected from hot cookware. Both materials can chip at exposed edges, especially around sinks and dishwashers.

Seams and layout

Slab size, pattern direction, cabinet layout and access determine seam locations. Review the slab layout before fabrication when pattern movement matters. A beautiful material can look disappointing if seams interrupt the design.

Repairs and long-term use

Small chips may be repairable, but results vary by color and location. Keep product information and leftover pieces when possible. Use cutting boards and trivets regardless of material.

ADELIE perspective

Do not choose from a small sample alone. View full slabs, consider the lighting in your home and coordinate the countertop with cabinet, backsplash and flooring samples together.
