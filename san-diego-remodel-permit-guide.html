Permit Guide

A Practical Guide to Remodeling Permits in San Diego County

Homeowner ResourceClear, practical guidance

Permit requirements depend on the project scope and the authority having jurisdiction. This guide helps homeowners recognize when permits may be involved and what to discuss before work begins. It is general planning information, not a substitute for confirmation from the applicable building department.

Identify the jurisdiction

San Diego County contains multiple cities plus unincorporated areas. The property address determines which building department reviews the work. Requirements, forms and timelines can differ, so confirm the jurisdiction first.

Understand common permit triggers

Structural changes, additions, new openings, plumbing relocation, electrical service changes, new circuits, HVAC work and alterations to life-safety systems often require permits. Cosmetic work may not, but the boundary depends on scope.

Determine the required documents

Simple work may use basic plans and product information. Structural changes can require architectural drawings, engineering, energy documentation or site information. Ask who prepares each item and who responds to plan-check comments.

Plan for review time

Permitting is a project phase. Allow time for document preparation, submission, corrections and approval. Do not order every custom item around an aggressive construction date until the permit path is understood.

Coordinate inspections

Inspections occur at specific stages before work is concealed. Missing an inspection can require removal of finished work. The construction schedule should identify likely inspection points and responsible parties.

Keep records

Retain approved plans, permits, inspection records and final documentation. These records can matter during future work, insurance questions or sale of the property.

ADELIE perspective

The permit conversation should happen during planning, not after demolition. Early confirmation protects the schedule and allows design choices to reflect actual code and documentation requirements.
